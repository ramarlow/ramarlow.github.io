My favorite project from UW's Engineering metal casting class was 3 replicas of an ancient Chinese wine vessel, which I cast in silicon brass using a lost PLA investment process. I found a [3D scan](https://theme.npm.edu.tw/3d/Content.aspx?sno=04001143&lang=2){:target="_blank"} of the original Zun wine vessel on display at Tawian's National Palace Museum. Using [Meshmixer](https://meshmixer.org/){:target="_blank"} I was able to get the polygon count down to a reasonable place without losing much fidelity, and I also sculpted the inside of the model to get a more uniform wall thickness and make the part castable.

The lost PLA process is effectively the same as a lost wax process, starting with a consumable positive that is then coated in ceramic and burned away to form the mold. The part geometry wasn't really great for printing or casting, so to minimize the post processing work I'd have to do later I spent a lot of time playing with print parameters and pushing the limits of what I could leave unsupported. To prevent infiltration of the liquid ceramic shell I made the prints as watertight as I could and also clear coated them a couple times. After a lot of sanding I took the best 3 PLA positives and attached gates in different locations to see which would end up working the best, then stuck them to the mold trees with everyone else's parts. 

I was glad to find that all my mold cavities filled with almost no defects. As expected, removing the gate marks from the casting took a pretty long time but the surface finish on the rest of the part ended up surprisingly good.
